Abstract

    Quantum computing has been gaining a lot of attention lately because researchers believe it could solve specific problems much faster than classical computers. 
  Instead of using standard bits, quantum computers use qubits, which follow the rules of quantum mechanics.
  This paper gives an overview of the basic ideas behind quantum computing—such as superposition and entanglement—and looks at a few major algorithms that have been proposed. 
  It also discusses some of the challenges the field faces right now, especially since most current devices are still noisy and limited. 
  Finally, a few potential applications in fields like cybersecurity, space exploration, and healthcare are explored. 
  Even though we are still far from having fully practical quantum computers, the progress being made suggests they could become very important in the future.

Definition

    Although classical computers have improved massively over the years, they still run into problems when dealing with extremely complex tasks. Simulating molecules or optimising high-dimensional systems can take far too long on classical machines. This is partly because classical bits must be either 0 or 1, which limits how much information can be represented at once.
Quantum computing processes information differently by using qubits, which can exist in multiple states simultaneously (Nielsen & Chuang). Because of properties such as superposition and entanglement, qubits can represent and process information in richer ways than classical bits.
We are currently in the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) era, where quantum computers exist but are still highly sensitive to noise (Nielsen & Chuang). Even though NISQ devices cannot yet solve significant real-world problems, they allow researchers to test early quantum algorithms and explore practical quantum machine learning ideas (Biamonte et al., 2017).
In this paper, I explain the main principles behind quantum computing, review several well-known quantum algorithms, and discuss the challenges that remain. I also describe some possible applications where quantum computing might make a big difference in the future.
